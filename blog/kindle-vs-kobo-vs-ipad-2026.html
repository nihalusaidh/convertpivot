Kindle vs Kobo vs iPad — Which E-reader Should You Buy in 2026?

July 13, 2026

I've been reading e-books for about fifteen years now. I started on a clunky Palm Pilot, moved through three different Kindles, spent a few years on a Kobo, and somewhere along the way I convinced myself an iPad mini was all I'd ever need. Spoiler: it wasn't.

Truth is, there's no perfect device. Every option forces you to make trade-offs — screen quality, format support, ecosystem, price. I've owned all three simultaneously at one point, which my wife described as "excessive but on-brand." She wasn't wrong.

Here's my honest take on each in 2026, based on actually living with them.

The Kindle Lineup

Amazon's current lineup runs from the basic Kindle ($99) to the Kindle Paperwhite ($149), the Kindle Scribe ($339 with pen), and the brand new Kindle Colorsoft ($279). All of them use E Ink Carta 1300 panels now — that's the latest display tech, and it's noticeably better than what you'd have gotten in 2023. The contrast is sharper, page turns are faster, and the front light is more even.

I have a Paperwhite Signature Edition that I've been using for about two years. It's fantastic for what it does. The battery lasts weeks, not hours. It fits in a jacket pocket. The screen is sharp enough that I forget I'm reading pixels. But here's the thing that bugs me: Amazon locks you into their ecosystem hard. You can't just drag an EPUB onto a Kindle. You've got to use Send to Kindle, or convert everything, or buy everything from Amazon. And Amazon knows this. They want you in the walled garden.

The Scribe is interesting if you take notes. I bought one thinking I'd use it for work. I didn't. It was too big to carry everywhere and the note-taking felt laggy compared to actual pen and paper. But for PDF reading — academic papers, reports — it's the best e-reader screen I've used. The Carta 1300 at 10.2 inches is gorgeous.

Kobo — The Reader's Reader

Kobo doesn't get the marketing budget Amazon does, but in my opinion, they make better devices for people who actually read. The Kobo Libra Colour ($219) uses a Kaleido 3 color E Ink screen. It's not iPad-level color, obviously. It's muted, like reading a newspaper with color photos. But for annotating, highlighting, and reading comics, it's genuinely useful.

I bought a Kobo Libra Colour last year after getting fed up with Amazon's format nonsense. The biggest difference? Kobo reads EPUB natively. You plug it into a computer, drag an EPUB file onto it, and it works. No conversion, no email-to-Kindle, no hassle. For someone like me who borrows library books via Libby and buys from multiple stores, that's huge.

The Kobo Sage ($259) is their premium black-and-white option with an 8-inch Carta 1300 screen and stylus support. If you don't need color, the Sage gives you more screen real estate than the Libra without the color layer that slightly dims the Kaleido screens. I've recommended the Sage to three friends and all of them still love it a year later.

The KLC (Kobo Libra Colour) has become my daily driver. It's not perfect — the battery lasts about two weeks instead of the Kindle's month — but the freedom to load any EPUB I want without jumping through hoops makes up for it. I keep a collection of about thirty books on it at any time, all in EPUB, all organized by series and author. It just works.

iPad — The Swiss Army Knife

I own an iPad mini 7th gen and a regular iPad 10th gen. The iPad mini is the one I'd consider as an e-reader. At 8.3 inches with a Liquid Retina display, it's gorgeous for comics, magazines, and PDFs. Color is vibrant. Video looks amazing. And you can install any reading app — Kindle, Kobo Books, Apple Books, Libby, Marvel Unlimited, you name it.

But here's the catch: reading on an iPad for more than an hour gives me eye strain. The LCD screen emits blue light directly into your face. Even with Night Shift, True Tone, and the brightness cranked down, it's not the same as the paper-like E Ink experience. I've fallen asleep reading on an iPad and woken up with a headache. Never happens with E Ink.

The iPad works great as a supplement, not a replacement. I use it for PDF-heavy technical books where color matters, for magazines, and for the Libby app when I'm too lazy to sync my Kobo. But for novel reading? I always reach for the Kobo or Kindle.

Format Support and Ecosystem Lock-In

This is where the decision gets real. Kindle uses AZW3 and KFX, both proprietary. You can't buy a book from Google Play Books or Kobo and put it on a Kindle without converting it. Amazon makes that conversion process just annoying enough that most people don't bother — they just buy from Amazon instead. And that's exactly the point.

Kobo reads EPUB and PDF natively. EPUB is the industry standard. Every library, every bookstore, every indie author publishes in EPUB. On a Kobo, you just drop the file on and go. No friction.

The iPad sits in a weird middle ground. It doesn't read anything natively — it reads everything through apps. Which is fine, but each app has its own library, its own sync system, its own quirks. I use Apple Books for purchased books, Kindle for Amazon purchases, and Libby for library books. It works but it's not seamless.

If you end up with books in the wrong format, you can always convert them. Which One Should You Buy?

If you're deep in the Amazon ecosystem — you have a Prime subscription, you buy all your books from Kindle, you use Audible — just get a Kindle. The integration is genuinely good. Whispersync between your phone and Kindle is seamless. You won't care about format support because you'll never need it.

If you're a more serious reader — you borrow library books, you buy from different stores, you have a collection of EPUB files — get a Kobo. The Kobo Libra Colour is the best all-around device for readers who value freedom over convenience. I bought one and I haven't looked back.

If you want a device that does everything — reading, browsing, video, notes, games — get an iPad. Just know that it's worse at reading than either E Ink device, and the battery life will force you to charge it daily. I keep my iPad mini for comics and PDFs, but for novels, it's always the Kobo.

Frequently Asked Questions

  1. Which is better for reading, Kindle or Kobo? Kobo wins on format flexibility and native EPUB support. Kindle wins on ecosystem and battery life. I switched from Kindle to Kobo and don't regret it.
  2. Can I use an iPad as an e-reader? Yes, but expect more eye strain and worse battery. Great for comics and PDFs, not great for long novel sessions.
  3. Does Kobo support EPUB natively? Yes, and it's the main reason to buy one over a Kindle. Drag and drop, no conversion needed.
  4. Which e-reader has the best screen in 2026? Kindle Scribe for black-and-white (10.2" Carta 1300). Kobo Libra Colour for color (Kaleido 3). Pick your priority.
  5. Can I convert e-books between Kindle and Kobo formats? Yes. Keep a master EPUB copy and convert as needed. Use Calibre for batch jobs or an online converter for quick single files.
