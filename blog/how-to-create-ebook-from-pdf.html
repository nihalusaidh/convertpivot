How to Create an E-book from PDF Files

July 13, 2026

A few years back, I downloaded a 300-page programming guide as a PDF. It looked fine on my laptop. But when I tried reading it on my phone during the commute, I spent more time pinching and zooming than actually reading. That's the PDF problem in a nutshell.

PDF is great for printing. For reading on a screen — especially a small one — it's a pain. That's why converting PDFs into proper e-book formats like EPUB makes so much sense. You get reflowable text, adjustable font sizes, and a reading experience that actually works on whatever device you're using.

Here's how to do it, from easiest to most powerful.

Method 2: Calibre (Desktop, Free)

Calibre is the heavyweight champion of e-book conversion. It's free, open-source, and runs on Windows, Mac, and Linux. If you're converting a whole library or need fine-grained control, this is the way to go.

To convert a PDF to an e-book in Calibre: add the PDF to your library, select it, click "Convert Books," and pick EPUB (or MOBI, AZW3, whatever) as the output. Calibre gives you pages and pages of options — font embedding, structure detection, table of contents generation, margin control, and more.

The catch? Calibre's PDF import works by extracting text and rebuilding it into the output format. That means if your PDF has complex formatting — columns, tables, floating images — the output might look weird. I've had PDFs with beautiful two-column layouts that turned into a jumbled mess in EPUB. You'll need to tweak the structure detection settings or clean up the output manually.

For straightforward PDFs — single column, clean text, standard headings — Calibre does a fantastic job. I've converted hundreds of Project Gutenberg PDFs this way and the results are excellent.

Method 3: Adobe Acrobat Pro

If you have Adobe Acrobat Pro (the paid version, not just the free reader), it has a built-in export to EPUB feature. Go to File > Export To > EPUB. You get a few options — include images, include bookmarks, cover image — and it generates an EPUB file.

Adobe's converter is decent but not perfect. It preserves more formatting than Calibre in some cases, especially for document-centric PDFs with headers, footers, and page numbers. The downsides: it costs money, it's slow for large files, and the output EPUB sometimes has weird spacing issues. I tried it once with a 400-page technical manual and the EPUB came out with random blank pages every few chapters. YMMV.

Method 4: Manual Conversion (Pandoc + Sigil)

For the control freaks out there — and I count myself among them — the best approach is to go manual. Use Pandoc to extract the text into clean HTML, then import it into Sigil (a free EPUB editor) for polish.

The command is simple: pandoc input.pdf -o output.html. Pandoc tries its best to extract structure from the PDF, marking up headings, paragraphs, and lists. The HTML won't be perfect, but it's a solid starting point. Then open that HTML in Sigil, split it into chapters, add a cover, fix any broken formatting, and export as EPUB.

This method takes longer — maybe thirty minutes for a book — but you get total control. I did this for a cookbook PDF that had terrible table layout, and after manually restructuring it in Sigil, the EPUB was better than anything the automatic tools produced.

Tips for Better Results

Not all PDFs convert equally. Here's what I've learned from converting hundreds of files:

Scan-based PDFs (images of pages, not selectable text) won't convert to usable EPUB unless you run OCR first. Tools like Adobe Acrobat Pro and Tesseract can handle OCR. Once you have actual text, conversion is straightforward.

PDFs with lots of tables, sidebars, or multi-column layouts need manual cleanup after conversion. Accept that going in and you won't be frustrated. For simple text documents — novels, reports, essays — automatic conversion works great.

Keep your original PDF. The EPUB will never be an exact replica because EPUB is reflowable and PDF is fixed. They serve different purposes. That's also why I keep ConvertPivot's EPUB to PDF converter bookmarked for going the other direction.

Frequently Asked Questions

  1. Why would I convert a PDF to an e-book format? Because reading PDFs on phones and e-readers is miserable. EPUB text reflows to fit your screen. Font gets bigger, pages adjust, no zooming required.
  2. Is converting PDF to EPUB free? Yes. ConvertPivot's online tool is free. Calibre is free. Pandoc is free. The only paid option is Adobe Acrobat Pro.
  3. Does formatting get preserved? Partially. Simple text converts well. Complex layouts with tables and columns need manual cleanup. EPUB and PDF are fundamentally different formats — reflowable vs fixed.
  4. What types of PDFs convert best? Single-column text PDFs. Novels, reports, essays. Anything with a clean, linear reading order. Avoid scanned documents without OCR.
  5. Can I read PDFs on a Kindle? Kindles can display PDFs but it's not a great experience. Convert to EPUB first (then to AZW3 if needed) for proper reflowable text with adjustable fonts.
