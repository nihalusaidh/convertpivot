E-book Formats Explained — EPUB, MOBI, AZW3, PDF, and More

July 13, 2026

When I bought my first Kindle back in 2011, I assumed all e-books were the same. You download a file, you read it on your device, done. Then I tried to open a library book on it and hit the wall that every new e-reader owner eventually hits: format incompatibility.

E-books come in a bunch of different formats, and they're not all created equal. Some are open standards. Some are proprietary locks designed to keep you inside a specific ecosystem. Some are great for novels but terrible for comics. Some are basically just images stuffed into a folder and renamed.

Let's break them down so you know what you're actually dealing with.

EPUB — The King of Formats

EPUB (Electronic Publication) is the closest thing e-books have to a universal standard. It's maintained by the W3C — the same people who run the web — and it works on every major e-reader except Kindle. Apple Books uses it. Google Play Books uses it. Kobo uses it. Your local library uses it for OverDrive and Libby loans.

What makes EPUB so good? It's built on web technology — XHTML and CSS. That means text reflows naturally when you change font size, rotate the screen, or switch devices. The file is just a ZIP container with all the content inside, which keeps file sizes small. A typical novel in EPUB format runs about 1-3 MB. Compare that to a PDF of the same book, which can be 10-15 MB.

EPUB supports embedded fonts, SVG graphics, MathML for equations, and rich metadata. It's also fully open — no DRM requirements, no proprietary restrictions, no vendor lock-in. That's why publishers love it and why Amazon refuses to support it natively on Kindles. (Though they now accept EPUB through Send to Kindle, which silently converts it.)

MOBI — The Legacy Format

MOBI started life at a company called Mobipocket, which Amazon acquired in 2005. For years, it was the native format for Kindle devices. It uses the Palm Database (PDB) container format — a relic from the PDA era — and supports basic reflowable text, Amazon's proprietary DRM, and limited formatting.

Amazon has been phasing out MOBI for years. Newer Kindles use AZW3 or KFX instead, which offer better typography, drop caps, and formatting options. But MOBI files are still everywhere on the internet. If you download a free e-book from a random site, it'll probably be MOBI.

I've got a folder of old MOBI files from my early Kindle days that I keep around mostly for nostalgia. They look dated compared to modern EPUBs — the formatting is basic, the font rendering is rougher — but they still work fine on any Kindle or the Kindle app.

AZW3 and KFX — Amazon's Modern Formats

AZW3, also called Kindle Format 8 or KF8, is Amazon's replacement for MOBI. It's essentially a MOBI container that holds EPUB-like content (HTML, CSS, embedded fonts). AZW3 supports more sophisticated formatting than MOBI — drop caps, justified text with hyphenation, embedded fonts, and better table rendering.

KFX is Amazon's latest format, introduced with the Kindle Oasis in 2016. It adds even more features — enhanced typesetting, panel-level page flip for comics, improved image handling, and Bookerly (Amazon's custom font). KFX files are typically only created when you buy a book from Amazon or use Send to Kindle. You can't easily generate KFX files yourself without Amazon's proprietary tools.

The problem with AZW3 and KFX? They're both proprietary. Amazon controls them completely. There's no open-source tool that can generate perfect KFX files. If Amazon decides to change the format tomorrow, every tool that converts to it might stop working. That's the risk of ecosystem lock-in.

PDF — The Print Standard

PDF is not really an e-book format. It's a document format that people use for e-books. And honestly, it's not great for that purpose. PDF pages are fixed — they're designed for a specific paper size, and they stay that way no matter what device you're viewing them on.

On a 24-inch monitor, a PDF looks fine. On a phone, you're pinching and zooming on every page. PDF also doesn't support reflowable text, so if you need larger fonts, you're out of luck. The file sizes are significantly larger than EPUB too.

That said, PDF excels where other formats fail. It preserves exact layout — crucial for textbooks, academic papers, manuals, and children's picture books. It's the standard format for print-on-demand services. And it's universally supported. Every computer, phone, and tablet can open a PDF without special software.

CBZ and CBR — Comic Book Formats

CBZ (Comic Book ZIP) and CBR (Comic Book RAR) aren't really e-book formats at all. They're collections of images — usually JPEG or PNG — compressed into an archive and renamed. CBZ uses ZIP compression, CBR uses RAR.

Comic readers like ComicRack, YACReader, and Kindle Comixology display these archives page by page, giving you a natural reading experience for comic books and manga. The image quality is whatever the original scan was — no reflow, no text adjustment.

I've got a CBZ collection of old Spider-Man comics that I read on my iPad mini. The image quality is perfect because it's just full-resolution page scans. No compression artifacts, no formatting issues. For comics, CBZ/CBR are the right choice every time.

DRM and Format Lock-In

Here's the thing nobody tells you when you buy your first e-reader: you don't actually own most of your e-books. You license them. And that license is enforced by DRM — Digital Rights Management — which ties the file to a specific ecosystem.

Buy a book from Amazon, it's locked to your Amazon account with Kindle DRM. Buy from Kobo, it's locked with Adobe DRM. You can't just trade files between devices unless you strip the DRM (which is legally questionable in most places) or stick to DRM-free sources.

My advice? Buy DRM-free whenever possible. Sites like Humble Bundle, Tor Books, and many indie publishers sell DRM-free EPUBs. You can read them on any device, back them up anywhere, and convert them whenever you want. Which Format Should You Use?

For most people, EPUB is the answer. It's open, widely supported, and handles 90% of what you'll want to read. If you're on Kindle, you can still get EPUB files and convert them or use Send to Kindle. For comics, use CBZ or CBR. For documents that need exact layout — contracts, textbooks, printables — use PDF.

And whatever format your books are in, keep a backup copy. Formats change. Devices die. Companies go out of business. A DRM-free EPUB on your hard drive will outlast any reader you buy.

Frequently Asked Questions

  1. What is the most common e-book format? EPUB. It's the industry standard for everything except Kindle. Open, flexible, and widely supported.
  2. What e-book format does Kindle use? Modern Kindles use AZW3 and KFX. Older ones used MOBI. Kindle now accepts EPUB via Send to Kindle with automatic conversion.
  3. What is the difference between MOBI and AZW3? AZW3 is the newer format with better typography and formatting. MOBI is the older, simpler format. Both are Amazon proprietary.
  4. What does CBZ and CBR mean? They're image archives for comics. CBZ is ZIP-based, CBR is RAR-based. Both store full-resolution page scans for comic reading apps.
  5. Can I convert between e-book formats? Yes, with the right tools. DRM-free books convert easily between formats. DRM-protected books are locked to their ecosystem.
