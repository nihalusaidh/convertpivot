MP4 vs MKV vs AVI vs MOV — Video Format Comparison

July 13, 2026

Video file formats can be confusing. With dozens of containers and codecs available, choosing the right format for your project matters more than most people realize. Whether you are editing a feature film, uploading to YouTube, or simply archiving your home videos, the choice between MP4, MKV, AVI, and MOV directly affects compatibility, file size, and quality.

Each of these four formats is a container — a digital wrapper that holds video, audio, subtitle tracks, and metadata. The quality of the video depends on the codec inside the container, but the container determines where and how that video can play. This video format comparison breaks down everything you need to know about MP4 vs MKV vs AVI vs MOV.

MP4 — The Universal Standard

MP4 (MPEG-4 Part 14) is the most widely supported video container format in the world. Every modern web browser, smartphone, smart TV, streaming platform, and media player supports MP4 playback natively. It uses the MPEG-4 compression standard and typically contains H.264 or H.265 video with AAC audio.

MP4 is the format of choice for online streaming. YouTube, Vimeo, Netflix, and social media platforms all recommend MP4 uploads. Its broad compatibility means you can share an MP4 file with anyone and be confident it will play without additional codecs or software. Hardware decoding support is built into virtually every device manufactured in the last decade.

The main limitations are its restricted support for advanced features like multiple audio tracks, embedded subtitles, and chapter markers. MP4 also has a practical 4 GB file size limit for standard playback, though this can be extended with the MP4-64 format.

MKV — The Feature-Rich Container

MKV (Matroska Video) is an open-source, royalty-free container format designed to hold an unlimited number of video, audio, subtitle, and metadata tracks in a single file. It is the preferred format for high-definition video archiving, Blu-ray rips, and any scenario where preserving every bit of quality matters.

MKV supports virtually any codec — H.264, H.265, VP9, AV1, MPEG-2, and even lossless codecs like FFV1. It handles subtitles in multiple formats (SRT, ASS, PGS) and supports chapter markers, attachments, and menu systems. This flexibility makes MKV the go-to format for the video enthusiast community.

MKV files often do not play natively on smart TVs, gaming consoles, or streaming devices. Most users rely on dedicated media players like VLC or Plex to handle MKV playback. For sharing finished videos with a general audience, converting MKV to MP4 is recommended for broader compatibility.

AVI — The Legacy Format

AVI (Audio Video Interleave) was introduced by Microsoft in 1992 and was the dominant video format throughout the 1990s and early 2000s. It uses a simple container structure that interleaves video and audio data in alternating chunks. AVI typically uses DivX or Xvid codecs for video and uncompressed PCM for audio, resulting in very large file sizes.

AVI has several technical limitations. It does not support modern video codecs like H.265 or VP9, cannot embed subtitles natively, and has a 2 GB file size limit with the standard format. The lack of a standardized index structure means that a partially downloaded AVI file may be completely unplayable.

AVI is largely obsolete for modern use. Most users who encounter AVI files today are dealing with legacy video archives. Converting AVI to MP4 using a modern codec like H.264 typically reduces file size by 60-80% while improving or maintaining visual quality. If you have a collection of old AVI files, converting them to MP4 or MKV is a worthwhile investment for long-term accessibility.

MOV — Apple's Multimedia Format

MOV (QuickTime File Format) was developed by Apple as the native format for its QuickTime multimedia framework. It supports a wide range of codecs, multiple audio and video tracks, chapter markers, and timecode data. MOV is the preferred format in professional video production workflows, particularly in Final Cut Pro and Adobe Premiere Pro.

MOV files support high bit depths (10-bit and 12-bit), alpha channels for transparency, and uncompressed audio — features essential for video editing and compositing. The format handles both progressive and interlaced content and supports closed captioning in various formats.

MOV is not as widely supported as MP4 on non-Apple devices and platforms. While VLC and modern media players handle MOV without issues, some smart TVs, gaming consoles, and web browsers may not play MOV files natively. For final delivery to a general audience, converting MOV to MP4 or uploading directly to streaming platforms is standard practice.

Which Video Format Should You Use?

Choose MP4 for: Universal compatibility, online streaming, social media uploads, email attachments, and any scenario where you need the recipient to play the file without downloading additional software. It is the safest choice for general video distribution.

Choose MKV for: Archiving high-quality video, storing Blu-ray or DVD rips, preserving multiple audio tracks and subtitle streams, and any situation where file size is less important than completeness and flexibility. MKV is the archivist's choice.

Choose AVI for: Legacy compatibility with old software and hardware, though migrating away from AVI is strongly recommended for modern use cases.

Choose MOV for: Professional video editing workflows, projects requiring alpha channels or high bit depth, and Apple ecosystem production pipelines. Convert to MP4 for distribution.

Frequently Asked Questions

  1. Which format should I use for YouTube? Upload MP4 with H.264 video and AAC audio. That is what YouTube recommends for the best balance of quality and processing speed.
  2. What is the real difference between MP4 and MKV? MP4 works everywhere — phones, TVs, browsers, game consoles. MKV is built for power users: unlimited audio tracks, subtitles, chapters, and any codec you want. Use MP4 for sharing, MKV for archiving.
  3. Can I still use AVI files? You can, but you probably should not. AVI lacks modern codecs, subtitle support, and has a 2 GB file limit. Convert AVI to MP4 and you will get a smaller file that actually plays on modern devices.
  4. When should I pick MOV over MP4? Pick MOV if you are editing video professionally — it handles higher bit depths, alpha channels, and uncompressed audio. Pick MP4 if you are sharing the final video with anyone else.
  5. Why will not my MKV files play on my smart TV? Most smart TVs do not support MKV natively. MKV is an open-source container that TV manufacturers rarely license. Convert MKV to MP4 for TV playback, or use a media player like VLC or Plex.
  6. Which format gives the smallest file size? MP4 with the H.265 (HEVC) codec gives the smallest file size for a given quality level. H.265 is roughly 50% more efficient than H.264, but not all devices support it.
  7. Is there a quality difference between these formats? The container itself does not affect quality. Quality depends on the codec and bitrate inside the container. A well-encoded MP4 can look identical to a well-encoded MKV with the same codec settings.
