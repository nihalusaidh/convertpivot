MP3 vs FLAC vs WAV vs OGG — Audio Format Comparison

July 13, 2026

Choosing the right audio format can be confusing. Whether you are building a music library, editing a podcast, or streaming tracks, each format serves a different purpose. This audio format comparison breaks down MP3, FLAC, WAV, and OGG — the four most common audio file formats — so you can pick the right one every time.

Lossy vs Lossless — What's the Difference?

Audio formats fall into two categories: lossy and lossless. Lossy formats (MP3, OGG) reduce file size by permanently removing audio data that the human ear is less likely to notice. This is called perceptual coding. Lossless formats (FLAC, WAV) preserve every bit of the original recording. WAV stores audio in raw PCM form with no compression at all, while FLAC uses mathematical compression to shrink files by about 40–50% without losing any information.

MP3 — The Universal Standard

MP3 (MPEG-1 Audio Layer 3) is the most widely supported audio format in the world. It uses lossy compression to reduce file sizes dramatically while retaining acceptable quality for most listeners. A 320 kbps MP3 is nearly indistinguishable from the original to most ears, making it perfect for portable music players, phone storage, and sharing. The format supports ID3 tags for rich metadata including album art, lyrics, and genre information. Virtually every device, operating system, and application supports MP3 playback.

FLAC — Lossless for Audiophiles

FLAC (Free Lossless Audio Codec) is the go-to format for audiophiles and music archivists. It compresses CD-quality audio to roughly half its original size without discarding any data. When you play a FLAC file, you hear an exact digital copy of the original recording. FLAC supports rich metadata via Vorbis comments, including embedded cover art. It is widely supported on Android, Linux, and modern music players, though native Apple support is limited. For anyone serious about audio quality, FLAC is the ideal format for a permanent music library.

WAV — The Studio Workhorse

WAV (Waveform Audio File Format) is the standard uncompressed audio format used in professional recording studios. It stores audio as raw PCM data, making it the largest format but also the most straightforward to work with. WAV files have almost no latency and require no decoding, which makes them ideal for audio editing, DAW projects, and sound design. The format's main limitations are file size (about 10 MB per minute of CD-quality stereo audio) and limited metadata support. WAV is also the format used for CD audio discs.

OGG — Open and Efficient

OGG Vorbis is a free, open-source lossy audio format that offers better quality than MP3 at the same bitrate. It uses variable bitrate encoding by default and supports up to 255 discrete channels, making it suitable for surround sound content. OGG is the standard audio format on the Steam gaming platform and is widely used in open-source multimedia projects. It supports Vorbis comments for rich metadata and is natively supported in HTML5 for web audio. OGG files are typically smaller than MP3 files at equivalent quality settings.

Which Format Should You Use?

For archiving: FLAC is the best choice. It preserves full quality while saving significant space compared to WAV. Archive your CD collection and master recordings in FLAC for a future-proof library.

For streaming: MP3 at 320 kbps or OGG at ~192 kbps (Spotify uses OGG at 320 kbps for its highest quality tier). Streaming favors smaller files with good enough quality.

For editing: WAV is the industry standard for DAWs and audio production. Its uncompressed nature means no decoding latency and no generation loss when re-encoding.

For portable devices: MP3 remains the safest choice for maximum compatibility across phones, car stereos, Bluetooth speakers, and smart home devices.

For gaming: OGG is the dominant format in PC gaming. Steam, game engines, and open-source projects all favor OGG for its royalty-free licensing and good compression.

Frequently Asked Questions

For the highest sound quality, WAV and FLAC are the best choices. WAV is uncompressed and preserves every bit of the original recording, making it ideal for professional audio editing. FLAC offers the same quality as WAV but at roughly half the file size thanks to lossless compression. If you need the absolute best quality, choose WAV for editing and FLAC for storage and playback.
FLAC is better than MP3 in terms of sound quality because it is a lossless format that preserves the full audio data from the original source. MP3 uses lossy compression that discards audio data to reduce file size. However, FLAC files are significantly larger than MP3 files. For casual listening on headphones or mobile devices, a 320 kbps MP3 is often indistinguishable from FLAC. For archival, home stereo systems, or professional use, FLAC is clearly superior.
iTunes and Apple Music do not natively support FLAC playback on desktop. However, you can convert FLAC to Apple Lossless (ALAC) for full integration with the Apple ecosystem. Alternatively, third-party players like VLC Media Player and Foobar2000 support FLAC on both Windows and macOS. On iOS, apps like VLC for Mobile and Flac Player+ can play FLAC files directly.
OGG (specifically OGG Vorbis) is a free, open-source audio format commonly used in gaming, streaming, and multimedia applications. It is the standard audio format for the Steam gaming platform, is used extensively in open-source projects like Wikipedia and Audacity, and is supported by HTML5 for web audio streaming. OGG offers better quality than MP3 at the same bitrate and has no patent restrictions.
MP3 at 128 kbps produces the smallest file size among these formats while maintaining acceptable audio quality for casual listening. A 4-minute song encoded as 128 kbps MP3 is roughly 3-4 MB. OGG at the same bitrate is slightly smaller or comparable. For maximum compression with reasonable quality, lossy formats like MP3 and OGG are the best choices. For context, the same song as WAV would be about 40 MB, and as FLAC about 20-25 MB.
