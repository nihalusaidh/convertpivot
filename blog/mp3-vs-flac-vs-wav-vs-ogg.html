MP3 vs FLAC vs WAV vs OGG Ś Audio Format Comparison

July 13, 2026

Let's be real Ś audio formats get confusing fast. Whether you're building a music library, editing a podcast, or just trying to figure out your setup, each format handles things differently. I've spent way too long comparing them, so here's the short version: MP3, FLAC, WAV, and OGG are the four you'll run into most, and this breakdown will help you pick the right one without the headache.

Frequently Asked Questions

WAV and FLAC, hands down. WAV is uncompressed so you get every bit of the original recording Ś perfect for professional editing. FLAC gives you the same quality at about half the size thanks to lossless compression. Use WAV for editing, FLAC for storage and playback.
Quality-wise, yes. FLAC is lossless so it preserves everything. MP3 throws data away to save space. That said, unless you've got high-end gear or golden ears, a good 320 kbps MP3 sounds basically identical to FLAC. For archiving or proper stereo setups, go FLAC. For casual listening, MP3 is fine.
Not natively, no. You can convert FLAC to Apple Lossless (ALAC) for full Apple ecosystem support. Or just use VLC or Foobar2000 Ś both play FLAC on Windows and macOS without any conversion.
OGG Vorbis is the open-source audio format you'll find in gaming (Steam uses it), open-source projects, and HTML5 web audio streaming. It actually sounds better than MP3 at the same bitrate and has zero patent headaches.
MP3 at 128 kbps. A 4-minute song comes in around 3-4 MB. OGG at the same bitrate is about the same. To put that in perspective, the same song as WAV would be about 40 MB, and as FLAC around 20-25 MB. If file size is your main concern, stick with lossy formats like MP3 or OGG.
