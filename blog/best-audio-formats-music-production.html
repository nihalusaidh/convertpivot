Best Audio Formats for Music Production Ś MP3 vs FLAC vs WAV vs AAC

July 13, 2026

I've been producing music for over a decade, and I still see people trip up over audio formats all the time. Pick the wrong one during recording, and you're stuck with artifacts you can't undo. Choose right at each stage Ś recording, mixing, mastering, distribution Ś and your final track sounds exactly how you intended.

In this guide, I'll walk through the four formats I actually use in the studio: WAV, FLAC, MP3, and AAC. We'll look at what makes each one tick, when to reach for them, and the real-world trade-offs between file size and quality. Frequently Asked Questions

WAV is the best format during recording, mixing, and mastering because it's uncompressed, preserves every bit of audio data, and has zero latency for real-time processing. Export masters as FLAC for lossless archiving, and convert to MP3 or AAC only for final distribution.
FLAC is better for storage and archiving Ś it cuts file size by 50-60% with zero quality loss and supports metadata natively. WAV is better for active production since it needs less processing to decode and works with every DAW out there.
Most listeners can't reliably tell 320 kbps MP3 from FLAC in blind listening tests, especially on consumer gear. On high-end headphones or studio monitors, some trained ears can pick up subtle differences.
44.1 kHz is the standard for CD-quality audio. 48 kHz is standard for film and video. 96 kHz gives you more headroom for processing and is preferred by some engineers for professional recording. Higher rates give diminishing returns.
Technically yes Ś AAC beats MP3 at the same bitrate, giving better quality at lower bitrates. AAC also supports more channels and higher sample rates. MP3 still wins on universal hardware compatibility, though.
