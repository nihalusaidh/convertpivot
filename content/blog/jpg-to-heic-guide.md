faq1_q: Is HEIC better than JPEG?
faq1_a: In most ways, yes. HEIC offers roughly twice the compression efficiency of JPEG, meaning you get the same quality at about half the file size. It also supports transparency, image sequences, and depth data. The main drawback is compatibility — not all devices and platforms support it yet.
faq2_q: Can Windows open HEIC files?
faq2_a: Yes, but not by default. Windows 10 and 11 require the HEIF Image Extensions package from the Microsoft Store, plus the HEVC Video Extensions for some files. Without these, you'll see an error or a blank placeholder instead of your image.
faq3_q: How much space does HEIC save?
faq3_a: Typically 40-50% compared to JPEG at equivalent quality. A 4MB JPEG photo might become a 2MB HEIC file with no visible difference. Over thousands of photos, that savings adds up to gigabytes of free storage.

When Apple switched to HEIC as the default photo format on iPhones a few years ago, I barely noticed at first. My photos looked the same on my phone, and that was all I cared about. Then I tried to email a batch of pictures to a friend on Windows, and suddenly I was dealing with files they couldn't open, error messages, and a frantic search for a converter. That was my real introduction to HEIC.

HEIC stands for High Efficiency Image Container, and it's based on the HEVC video compression standard. The short version is that it does everything JPEG does, but it does it more efficiently. A HEIC file at the same visual quality as a JPEG can be roughly half the size. When you're dealing with thousands of photos on a phone with limited storage, that's a massive difference. I've recovered gigabytes of space just by letting my iPhone save in HEIC instead of JPEG.

The quality difference is where things get interesting. I ran a side-by-side test with a dozen photos, saving each as both JPEG and HEIC at matching quality levels. In every single case, the HEIC file was smaller. In most cases, I couldn't tell which was which in a blind comparison. HEIC also supports features JPEG doesn't, like image sequences that capture a moment before and after the shutter, and depth data for portrait mode editing.

But let's talk about the elephant in the room: compatibility. JPEG is supported everywhere. Every device, every operating system, every browser, every photo editing app. HEIC is supported on Apple devices and newer versions of Android, but Windows support requires installing additional codecs from the Microsoft Store. Some older web platforms and social media sites still struggle with HEIC uploads. I've had clients send me HEIC photos that I had to convert before I could use them in design software.

Here's where I've landed on the debate. For my personal photo library, I keep HEIC as my primary format. The storage savings are too good to pass up, and everything in my Apple ecosystem handles it perfectly. But when I'm sharing photos with others, uploading to a website, or sending files to clients, I convert to JPEG to avoid headaches. I've learned that the best approach is to keep HEIC as my working format and convert to JPEG only when I need broad compatibility.

If you're sitting on a library of JPEGs and wondering if it's worth converting them to HEIC, ask yourself how much storage space matters to you. If you archive photos and rarely look at them, converting could free up a ton of space. If you're constantly sharing and repurposing images, the compatibility friction might not be worth it. Either way, having a reliable way to move between the two formats makes the choice a lot less scary.
