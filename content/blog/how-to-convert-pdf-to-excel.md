I deal with PDF documents all the time — invoices, bank statements, financial reports, you name it. PDFs are fine for sharing but useless when you need to actually crunch the numbers. You want that data in a spreadsheet where you can run formulas, build charts, and filter rows.

Converting PDF to Excel does not have to be complicated or cost anything. I have tried dozens of methods over the years, and here are the ones that actually work without spending a dime.


Can I convert PDF to Excel for free? ▼Yes. ConvertPivot is free, Excel can open PDFs directly, and LibreOffice Calc handles it too. No payment needed.Does it work with scanned PDFs? ▼It depends on the scan quality. Clean scans at 300 DPI or higher work well. Blurry or handwritten documents are hit or miss.Why does my converted data look different from the PDF? ▼Excel preserves the data, not the visual layout. Column widths, fonts, and spacing will differ. You might need to adjust formatting after conversion.Can I convert password-protected PDFs? ▼Most free tools cannot handle password-protected PDFs. Remove the password first or use Adobe Acrobat Pro.Is my data safe with online converters? ▼With ConvertPivot, yes — everything stays in your browser. With upload-based converters, your file goes to their server, so avoid using those for sensitive documents.
